## S1: Analysis of Shapes Illustrating the Closure Aspect of the Gestalt Principle of Visual Perception

The closure aspect of the Gestalt Principle of Visual Perception emphasizes our tendency to perceive incomplete shapes as complete forms. This principle illustrates how our perceptual system fills in missing portions of visual figures, allowing us to interpret them as whole objects despite their fragmented nature.

### Shapes Used to Illustrate Closure

1. **Circle Composed of Line Segments**: This shape consists of 12 evenly spaced black line segments that are intentionally left with gaps. Despite these interruptions, our brain interprets this arrangement as a complete circle. This phenomenon exemplifies how our perception can unify disjointed elements into a singular form, demonstrating the closure principle effectively.

2. **Partially Obscured Circular Arcs**: Another example is a minimalist line drawing featuring circular arcs that are partially obscured. Viewers tend to perceive this as a complete circle, even though parts of the arcs are missing. This further illustrates the closure principle, as our minds automatically fill in the gaps to create a coherent whole.

3. **Incomplete Circle with Gaps**: This shape is characterized by an incomplete circular outline, which also contains gaps. Similar to the previous examples, our perception leads us to see this incomplete figure as a complete circle, showcasing the brain's ability to fill in visual gaps.

These shapes collectively demonstrate the closure aspect of the Gestalt Principle of Visual Perception, highlighting our innate ability to perceive completeness in the presence of visual discontinuities. This principle is fundamental in understanding how we interpret visual information and the cognitive processes involved in perception.
