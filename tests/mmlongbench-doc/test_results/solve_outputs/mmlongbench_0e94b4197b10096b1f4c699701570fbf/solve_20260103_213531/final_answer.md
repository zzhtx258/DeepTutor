## S1: Historical Context and Significance of Gestalt Psychology's Origin

Gestalt psychology was conceived in the early 20th century, primarily within the Berlin School of Experimental Psychology. This movement emerged as a significant response to the prevailing schools of thought at the time, particularly behaviorism and structuralism. While behaviorism focused on observable behaviors and structuralism aimed to break down mental processes into their most basic components, Gestalt psychology shifted the focus towards understanding how humans perceive and organize sensory information into meaningful wholes.

The term "Gestalt," which translates from German to "shape" or "form," encapsulates the core principle of this psychological approach: perceptions are fundamentally different from the sum of their parts. This idea emphasizes that our cognitive processes are holistic, meaning that we tend to perceive entire structures rather than merely a collection of individual elements. For example, when viewing a complex image, our brains instinctively group visual elements based on certain laws, leading to a unified perception rather than a fragmented one.

This holistic processing is articulated through the Gestalt Laws of Grouping, which explain how we organize visual information. These laws include principles such as proximity, similarity, closure, and continuity, which guide our perception and help us make sense of the visual world around us. By focusing on the relationships among visual elements, Gestalt psychology has profoundly influenced various fields, including cognitive psychology, design, and art, by highlighting the importance of context and organization in perception.

In summary, the origin of Gestalt psychology within the Berlin School of Experimental Psychology marks a pivotal moment in the history of psychology, as it introduced a new perspective that emphasizes the significance of holistic perception and the inherent organization of sensory information. This approach not only challenged existing paradigms but also laid the groundwork for future research in understanding human cognition and perception.
